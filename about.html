About the Lab

The Unmapped Urban Futures Lab was created to study a central problem in contemporary planning: how can we understand urban change by learning from the communities most affected by it? In many cities, the people who live with the consequences of development are also the people least represented in official planning processes and public narratives. Our lab asks what becomes visible when we take those communities’ analyses, histories, and organizing practices seriously as forms of knowledge.

Our Approach

The lab brings together two complementary traditions of research. One is grounded in qualitative and community-engaged methods: oral histories, archival research, and participant observation. The other draws on quantitative analysis and large-scale spatial data to examine patterns of change across neighborhoods, jurisdictions, and regions. Working across these approaches allows us to connect lived experience with broader structural conditions, and to study both the everyday and the systemic dimensions of urban inequality.

Our Areas of Focus

Our shared research interests span housing, transportation, land use, and community development. We are especially interested in how these fields intersect in the lives of residents navigating displacement, gentrification, mobility, and uneven development. We approach each of these areas as interconnected parts of the urban landscape and of the political struggles that shape it.

Our Training Environment

The lab is also a training space for students. We aim to prepare undergraduate and graduate researchers to work across methods while thinking carefully about ethics, representation, and the responsibilities of research. Students in the lab learn how to analyze data, as well as how to ask better questions about whose knowledge counts, how cities are narrated, and what it means to conduct research in partnership with communities.

Why it Matters

At its core, the Unmapped Urban Futures Lab is a place for collaborative inquiry. We are interested in how communities build alternative infrastructures of planning, governance, and care, and in how research can help document, interpret, and support those efforts.
